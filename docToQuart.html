BACKGROUND INFO

STEAM -ELECTRIC POWER

A Steam-Electric Power Station is a powerstation that makes use of a steam engine{<sourcedoc: “steam turbine”>}. These steam engines take in heat energy and convert it into mechanical motion by boiling water. This mechanical motion is then converted into electricity by generator. 

While using a steam engine for power may seem old fashioned, modern steam turbines are much different that the kind that old steam trains used, and are much more efficient. A typical coal power plant in the year 1900 would be less than 5% efficient, while a modem steam power plant usually achieve efficiencies over 32% and a combined cycle natural gas plant can go well over 40% {<sourcedoc: primary to final>}{<sourcedoc:heat rates>}.

These steam turbines are critical components of the world’s power infrastructure, as most of the world’s electricity is generated using them {<sourcedoc: steam turbine>}, and 53.2% of US electricity is generated using a steam turbine  {<sourcedoc: steam turbine>}. These turbines make use of a large variety of heat sources: coal, natural gas, nuclear, biomass, and even solar power are used to drive steam turbines. While steam turbines are part of a large share of the electricity generated, there are many other technologies achieving widespread use that don’t require steam turbines. These alternatives will be discussed individually in the @sources of power@ section below.

SOLAR POWER

Solar power is energy derived from energy directly produced by the sun. There are two main types:

Concentrated

Concentrated solar power plants collect the energy from the sun’s rays into a small area using mirrors, to create heat (similar to using a magnifying glass on a bright day to start fires){<CSP>}. This heat can then be used to drive a @generator@{<CSP>}. 

Photovoltaic

Photovoltaic solar energy, also called solar panels, use semiconductors to directly convert light into electricity without any moving parts (<solarcellphysics>). Photovoltaic panels output minimal power at night, and are considered an intermittent form of power that can benefit from energy storage systems {<intermittent>}.

HYDRO ELECTRIC

Hydroelectric power is power extracted from water flowing from a higher point of altitude to a lower point of altitude {<con hydro>} {<pumped hydro>}. There are two main kinds of hydroelectric power:

Conventional

Conventional hydroelectric power uses naturally occurring water sources as a way to generate electricity{<con hydro>} . These water sources can replenish themselves via the water cycle, and shouldn’t need to be refilled{<con hydro>} . 

Pumped

Pumped hydrolectric power uses water from reservoirs that are filled using mechanical pumps {<pumped hydro>}. These pumps require electricity, and are usually powered when energy is  in lower demand{<pumped hydro>}. This allows the reservoir to extract that energy when demand goes up{<pumped hydro>}. Pumped hydro power is not a method of generating new energy into the grid, and is more so a method of storing energy that has already been generated{<pumped hydro>}. Energy storage systems can help increase the reliability of the grid, especially when intermittent generators (such as solar and wind power) are in use {(ph renew)}{<intermittent>}. Pumped hydro power is currently the largest source of energy storage in the United States, surpassing other forms of storage, such as large scale batteries {<pumped hydro>}.

WIND POWER

Wind power is a form of electricity extracted from the motion of the wind, most commonly by using large generators called wind turbines. There are two main types of wind power:

Onshore

Onshore wind turbines are wind generators built on land – typically in areas that are not densely populated {<adv and challenges>}{<wind>}. The output of an onshore wind turbine is dependent on the strength of the wind in the area, thus a wind turbine is considered an intermittent form of power, and can benefit from power storage systems {<intermittent>}

Offshore

Offshore wind turbines are wind generators build off-land, usually on the ocean nearby the coasts.These turbines can potentially offer more powerful and consistent electricity than onshore wind, but are more dificult to establish and maintain {<wind>}{<offshore wind>}{<offshore article>}.
