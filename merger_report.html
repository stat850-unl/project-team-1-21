PURPOSE

In the recent past and for many today, the sole concern when talking about electricity supply was reliability: If I flip the switch, do my lights turn on every single time. As consumers, reliability is paramount, but there have been growing concerns about other factors, and new ways of generating electricity have set many power sources apart from the rest. Concerns about mitigation of pollution have also become more pressing as information about the potential harm of power generation has become more widely available. Grid power is more complicated, and the audience is more engaged than ever.

Being an informed consumer and voter is difficult task when topic is complicated, and power infrastructure is a deeply technical and interconnected topic. We would like to provide some helpful information to people interested in learning more about energy sources and their characteristics, but there will be no conclusion, and no final winner. Whether or not one power source is better than another depends largely on there area and time of construction, how it is operated, and the values of the consumers. As such, this report will only discuss individual characteristics, and not use those characteristics to form any recommendation.

ABOUT THE DATA

The Energy Information Agency (EIA) is an agency of the Federal Statistical System of the United States, and the agency collects, analyzes, and publishes information about energy systems. The EIA is intended to work independently of public policy decisions, and does not directly advocate for specific policies.

The EIA hosts an Open Data program with the stated intention of making energy data \“more accessible, understandable, relevant and responsive\”\(^{1}\). As part of the Open Data program, individuals are allowed to request large datasets containing information on generators across the country\(^{1}\). Requesting this information free, provided the person obtains a free API key to make the request. For the data used in this analysis, a request was made for 5000 entries for each 8 months in the timeframe of January 2023 to August 2023 with a total of 40,000 entries used in our analysis.

For more information on obtaining the information for reproducibility, please go to the Reproducibility and Code section.

BACKGROUND INFO

Steam -Electric Power

A Steam-Electric Power Station is a powerstation that makes use of a steam engine\(^{18}\). These steam engines take in heat energy and convert it into mechanical motion by boiling water. This mechanical motion is then converted into electricity by generator. 

While using a steam engine for power may seem old fashioned, modern steam turbines are much different that the kind that old steam trains used, and are much more efficient. A typical coal power plant in the year 1900 would be less than 5% efficient, while a modem steam power plant usually achieve efficiencies over 32% and a combined cycle natural gas plant can go well over 40% \(^{[28][29]}\).

These steam turbines are critical components of the world’s power infrastructure, as most of the world\’s electricity is generated using them \(^{[18]}\), and 53.2% of US electricity is generated using a steam turbine  \(^{[18]}\). These turbines make use of a large variety of heat sources: coal, natural gas, nuclear, biomass, and even solar power are used to drive steam turbines. While steam turbines are part of a large share of the electricity generated, there are many other technologies achieving widespread use that don’t require steam turbines. These alternatives will be discussed individually in the “Background: Power Types” section below.

Categorization

Many of the metrics in this report are based on the use of fuel (e.g. the cost of fuel to generate a unit of energy), but not all sources of electricity require the operator to input fuel. For example, solar panels use the sun’s rays to generate electricity, so there is no need to “top up” the panels with fuel. This makes concepts such as the sulfur content of the fuel and the cost per unit of the fuel a bit nonsensical, because there is no fuel to begin with.

To organize the data for convenient reading, the power sources were put into two groups, fuelees and non-fuelees. Fuelees are power sources that require fuel to be regularly put in the generator to make power. Examples include: coal, natural gas, biomass, and nuclear. Non-fuelees, on the other hand, do not require regular input fuel to operate (although they may require regular maintenance). Examples include: wind, solar, and hydro-electric power.

It is worth noting that while solar panels do technically require fuel to generate power, the fuel in question is fusion in the sun, which provides that energy free of charge. While it would be possible to do a similar analysis on the sun’s fusion, this would not produce practical information in regards to power generation, as cost per unit of hydrogen and the sulfur content in the sun are not relevant to the normal use of solar panels on Earth. For this reason, this form of analysis was not included in the report. 

Background: Power Types

Coal Power

Coal power is generated by burning coal to generate heat which powers a steam electric generator. Coal is a combustible organic sedimentary rock composed of a large fraction of carbon, with several different types that can be burned in coal power plants \(^{[5]}\). The types of coal used for power are often differentiated by percentage of the stones mass which is made of carbon as well as how the coal was produced\(^{[5]}\).

Among the different types of coal power in the data set are the naturally occurring anthracite, bituminous, subbituminous, and lignite coal. The percentage of carbon varies for each with anthracite containing 86%-97%, bituminous containing 45%-86%, subbituminous containing 35%-45% carbon, and lignite containing 25%-65% carbon.  

Not all coal included in the data set are naturally occurring. One of the included unnatural forms of coal is waste coal. Waste coal is a term for the usable material that is generated as a byproduct of coal processing. In addition to coal, this material contains soil and mine waste. This can be burned using specific machines and can sometimes be cleaned by removing some of the non-combustible materials \(^{[15]}\)

Another unnatural coal in the data set is refined coal. Refined coal (not to be confused with coal that has standard pre-processing) is coal that has been modified to reduce impurities and improve heat content \(^{[16]}\). Refined coal meets the standards set by the IRS definition of the substance{\<notice 2010-54\>}. Refined coal is often made by mixing proprietary additives into coal \(^{[35]}\).

Flammable Gas Power

Natural gas power is generated by burning gas reserves found underground. The gas is highly flammable and is composed mostly of methane\(^{[21]}\). This is different from landfill gas, which occurs naturally when bacteria naturally decomposes organic materials into a gas that is approximately 50% methane and 50% carbon dioxide. This gas can be treated to remove carbon dioxide and other gasses, turning it into biomethane, or burned directly for energy.

Biomass

Biomass is organic material derived from living organisms. Biomass can include wood, grasses, foods, excrement, and biogenic portions of municipal solid waste\(^{[4]}\). Biomass can be burned directly or turned into other useful products such as methane or chemical feedstocks\(^{[4][37][38]}\).

Biogenic Municipal Solid Waste

Municipal solid waste is the formal term for garbage. Municipal solid waste can contain a mixture of organic flammable compounds and non-combustible materials\(^{[37]}\). The biogenic municipal solid waste is the parts of garbage that is produced by living organisms. Examples of this include discarded food, paper, cardboard. \(^{[4]}\).Municipal solid waste can be burned directly in waste to energy plants converted to combustible gas at landfills, or converted into other chemical products \(^{[38]}\)\(^{[4]}\).\(^{[37]}\)

Solar Power

Solar power is energy derived from energy directly produced by the sun. There are two main types: concentrated and photovoltaic. Concentrated solar power plants collect the energy from the sun’s rays into a small area using mirrors, to create heat, which is similar to using a magnifying glass on a bright day to start fires \(^{[6]}\). This heat can then be used to drive a generator\(^{[6]}\). 

Photovoltaic solar energy, also called solar panels, use semiconductors to directly convert light into electricity without any moving parts \(^{[31]}\). Photovoltaic panels output minimal power at night, and are considered an intermittent form of power that can benefit from energy storage systems \(^{[39]}\).

Hydro Electric

Hydroelectric power is power extracted from water flowing from a higher point of altitude to a lower point of altitude \(^{[19]}\) \(^{[20]}\). There are two main kinds of hydroelectric power. The first is conventional hydroelectric power which uses naturally occurring water sources as a way to generate electricity\(^{[19]}\) . These water sources can replenish themselves via the water cycle, and shouldn’t need to be refilled\(^{[19]}\). 

The second form of hydroelectric power in our data set is pumped. Pumped hydroelectric power uses water from reservoirs that are filled using mechanical pumps \(^{[20]}\). These pumps require electricity, and are usually powered when energy is  in lower demand\(^{[20]}\). This allows the reservoir to extract that energy when demand goes up\(^{[20]}\). Pumped hydro power is not a method of generating new energy into the grid, and is more so a method of storing energy that has already been generated\(^{[20]}\). Energy storage systems can help increase the reliability of the grid, especially when intermittent generators (such as solar and wind power) are in use \(^{[22]}\)\(^{[39]}\). Pumped hydro power is currently the largest source of energy storage in the United States, surpassing other forms of storage, such as large scale batteries \(^{[20]}\).

Wind Power

Wind power is a form of electricity extracted from the motion of the wind, most commonly by using large generators called wind turbines. There are two main types of wind turbines depending on the location. If the turbines are built inland, they are most likely onshore wind turbines. Onshore wind turbines are built on land and typically in areas that are not densely populated \(^{[0][40]}\). The output of an onshore wind turbine is dependent on the strength of the wind in the area, thus a wind turbine is considered an intermittent form of power, and can benefit from power storage systems \(^{[39]}\).

Conversely, offshore wind turbines are wind generators built off-land, usually on the ocean near the coasts. These turbines can potentially offer more powerful and consistent electricity than onshore wind, but are more difficult to establish and maintain \(^{[40][26][7]}\).

Petroleum Power

Petroleum describes a series of products made from crude oil \(^{[27]}\). These products can vary significantly, but in the context of this document we will focus on three main petroleum products: distillate fuel oil, residual fuel oil, and petroleum coke.

Distillate fuel oil is a liquid product obtained from distilling crude oil \(^{[27]}\). Distillate fuel oil has a higher boiling point than gasoline and kerosine, and is often used in heating oil and diesel fuel\(^{[27][14]}\).

Residual fuel oil is a liquid product obtained from distilling crude oil  \(^{[27]}\). With a boiling point of greater than 1,050F, this fuel can be burned in steam boats and powerplants or mixed with distillate fuel oil to be used in certain types of diesel engines \(^{[14]}\). 

Petroleum coke is a combustible, carbon-based, solid material obtained during petroleum refining operations \(^{[2]}\). Despite being considered a waste byproduct\(^{[36]}\). It can be used in power generation, metal refining, and cement production \(^{[2]}\).


Waste Oils and Other Oils ##look into this one in particular

Similar to petroleum coke, waste oil is oil that is produced as a byproduct of some industrial processes \(^{[36]}\). These oils can still be burned for heating and energy purposes \(^{[36]}\).

Geothermal Power

Geothermal power is energy obtained from the natural heat given off by the internals of the earth \(^{[13]}\). Conventional geothermal power is currently restricted in where it can be effectively and safely constructed.

Nuclear Power

Nuclear power is energy obtained from heat generated by splitting large atomic nuclei \(^{[24]}\). Nuclear power plants typically rely on enriched uranium fuel \(^{[25]}\), although some forms of reactors do not require enriched uranium and can use other fuels \(^{[8]}\)

Thermal Efficiency

Thermal efficiency is the ratio of energy used in a generator vs the amount of useful energy produced. For example, if you put in 10 units of energy, and get back 5 units of electricity, the thermal efficiency is 50%. Thermal efficiency is, to some, easier to understand than the heat rate. It can be used in other calculations, some of which will be mentioned further in the document. Below is Graph 2, which charts the available and sensible power types and their thermal efficiencies. Solid waste has been shown to be the least efficient of the relevant types, below 20%. Natural gas was shown to be significantly more thermally efficient than most other power sources, with a thermal efficiency over 40%. This higher efficiency is partially due to the different types of generators used in natural gas power plants \(^{[34]}\). These generators, called combined cycle power plants, use multiple heat engines together to extract more energy during combustion\(^{[34]}\). This arrangement is primarily used for natural gas, which contributes to the large spike in efficiency for natural gas systems\(^{[34]}\).

While the cost of fuel could be measured in kilograms, barrels, gallons, or any other physical unit, this may not be a fair measurement. The amount of energy in a kilogram of fuel can vary dramatically, so to account for those differences, the cost of fuels was measured by the amount of energy in dollars per million Btu. Naturally mined coal, of all kinds, displays a large sulfur content here. Interestingly, the sulfur content of petroleum is modest, but when split into petroleum coke and petroleum liquids, the petroleum coke has some of the highest sulfur content values, while the petroleum liquids have some of the lowest sulfur content values. The process of refining petroleum seems to isolate the high sulfur content substances from the lower sulfur content substances. This is likely a good thing, as vehicles without much sulfur scrubbing protection burn petroleum liquids, while more specific power plants tend to burn petroleum coke.

Unsurprisingly, refined coal (coal which has been processed to remove impurities and improve heat content) has some of the lowest values for sulfur content.
